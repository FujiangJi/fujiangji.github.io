    Ji, F.; Yang, J.; Townsend, P. A.; Zheng, T.; Kovach, K. R.; Yu, T.; Yang, R.; Liu, M.; Chen, M.

    Robust hyperspectral reconstruction from satellite and airborne observations via a deep hierarchical fusion network across heterogeneous scenarios
    Remote Sensing of Environment 2026, 338, 115385 First Author IF 13.5 JCR Q1
    Abstract

    High spatial resolution hyperspectral imagery (HR-HSI) is essential for fine-scale ecological and environmental monitoring, yet current spaceborne sensors are constrained by a trade-off between spectral and spatial resolution. This study develops a deep learning fusion framework that reconstructs HR-HSI by integrating low-resolution hyperspectral imagery from NASA's EMIT with high-resolution multispectral imagery from PlanetScope. The model was evaluated across three ecologically distinct landscapes in the western United States using airborne AVIRIS-NG and AVIRIS-3 imagery as reference data. The proposed framework consistently outperformed seven peer state-of-the-art fusion models, achieving a best spectral angle mapper (SAM) of 2.64 and a root mean squared error (RMSE) of 0.0267. A sensor inter-calibration pipeline ensured radiometric consistency between EMIT and PlanetScope, improving the reliability of the fusion inputs.

    Ji, F.; Zheng, T.; Shiklomanov, A. N.; Yang, R.; Townsend, P. A.; Li, F.; Hao, D.; Dashti, H.; Kovach, K. R.; You, H.; Zhou, J.; Chen, M.

    Tracking seasonal variability in plant traits from spaceborne PRISMA and NEON AOP across forest types and ecoregions
    Remote Sensing of Environment 2026, 333, 115149 First Author IF 13.5 JCR Q1
    Abstract

    Tracking seasonal dynamics of plant functional traits is critical for understanding ecosystem responses to climate variability, yet capturing these dynamics at landscape scales remains challenging. This study leveraged the PRISMA spaceborne hyperspectral sensor to monitor seasonal variability of four key plant traits—leaf chlorophyll content (Cab), equivalent water thickness (EWT), leaf mass per area (LMA), and leaf area index (LAI)—across 11 NEON forest sites spanning diverse forest types and ecoregions across the contiguous United States. We developed a workflow integrating PROSAIL radiative transfer model simulations with machine learning algorithms, achieving robust trait retrieval validated against concurrent NEON AOP airborne hyperspectral estimates (R² = 0.813–0.88 across all traits). Results revealed distinct seasonal trajectories, with deciduous broadleaf forests exhibiting the most pronounced dynamics and evergreen needleleaf forests showing more muted responses. Substantial cross-site and cross-season variation in trait magnitudes highlighted the importance of ecoregion context. These findings demonstrate the potential of spaceborne imaging spectroscopy for characterizing plant trait seasonality at ecosystem scales and provide a foundation for future global trait monitoring efforts.

    Leveraging transfer learning and leaf spectroscopy for leaf trait prediction with broad spatial, species, and temporal applicability
    Remote Sensing of Environment 2025, 326, 114818 First Author IF 13.5 JCR Q1
    Abstract

    Accurate prediction of leaf functional traits from spectroscopic measurements is essential for large-scale ecosystem monitoring, yet most existing models suffer from limited transferability across different locations, species, and seasons. This study presents a physics-guided transfer learning framework that leverages a large, diverse global leaf spectral library combined with radiative transfer model simulations to pre-train deep learning encoders, then fine-tunes them on target-domain data. The approach substantially improved trait prediction accuracy across spatial, species, and temporal boundaries compared to conventional PLSR and deep learning baselines. By embedding physical knowledge of leaf optics into the learning process, the model generalizes effectively with minimal site-specific calibration data, offering a scalable solution for leaf trait mapping from airborne and spaceborne imaging spectroscopy.

    Ji, F.*; Li, F.; Hao, D.; Shiklomanov, A. N.; Yang, X.; Townsend, P. A.; Dashti, H.; Nakaji, T.; Kovach, K. R.; Liu, H.; Luo, M.; Chen, M.

    Unveiling the transferability of PLSR models for leaf trait estimation: lessons from a comprehensive analysis with a novel global dataset
    New Phytologist 2024, 243: 111–131 First Author · Corresponding Author IF 8.3 JCR Q1 ESI Top 1% Highly Cited
    Abstract

    Partial least squares regression (PLSR) models demonstrate commendable performance in predicting leaf functional traits—including chlorophyll content, carotenoids, leaf water, and leaf mass per area—within their original training datasets. However, performance degrades significantly when applied to new contexts. Using a novel global leaf spectral dataset assembled for this study, we systematically evaluated PLSR transferability across new locations, seasons, and plant functional types. When extrapolating beyond training conditions, models showed substantially reduced accuracy (R² = 0.12–0.49 for new locations, 0.15–0.42 for new seasons, and 0.25–0.56 for new plant functional types) alongside increased error rates (NRMSE = 3.58–33.12%). The study emphasizes the importance of incorporating greater spectral diversity during model training to improve transferability, and highlights potential systematic errors when applying leaf trait estimates across large spatial scales, diverse vegetation types, and different time periods.

    Crop Yield Estimation at Field Scales by Assimilating Time Series of Sentinel-2 Data into a Modified CASA-WOFOST Coupled Model
    IEEE Trans. Geosci. Remote Sens. 2021, PP(99): 1–14 First Author IF 8.2 JCR Q1
    Abstract

    Accurate field-scale crop yield estimation is essential for precision agriculture and food security assessment. This study developed a data assimilation framework that integrates time series Sentinel-2 satellite observations into a modified CASA-WOFOST coupled model, combining the carbon assimilation capabilities of CASA with the process-based crop growth simulation of WOFOST. The approach sequentially assimilated leaf area index and canopy light absorption retrieved from Sentinel-2 imagery to constrain model state variables throughout the growing season. Evaluated across multiple field sites for winter wheat and summer maize, the framework achieved substantially improved yield predictions compared to either model run independently, demonstrating the value of satellite data assimilation for operational crop monitoring at agricultural management scales.

    A Quantitative Assessment of the Inconsistency Between Waterbody Segmentation and Shoreline Positioning in Deep Learning Models
    Geomatics 6(1), 21 · 2025 Corresponding Author IF 2.3 JCR Q2
    Abstract

    This study evaluates the inconsistency between deep learning-based waterbody segmentation and shoreline positioning using aerial imagery from the Great Lakes region. Despite strong pixel-level segmentation accuracy (>98% accuracy, >90% IoU) across five benchmark models tested on 20,689 NAIP images, shoreline positioning accuracy was substantially lower and varied significantly by landscape type—with poorest results in wetlands and urban areas. The shoreline-to-water ratio emerged as the dominant predictor of positioning accuracy, while shoreline complexity and water color showed weaker, context-dependent effects. These findings highlight the need for shoreline-specific evaluation metrics rather than relying solely on conventional segmentation benchmarks when applying deep learning to coastal and lake boundary detection tasks.

    Study on Soybean Yield Estimation Using the Coupled CASA and WOFOST Model
    Remote Sensing Technology and Application 2020, 35(2): 406–415 First Author IF 1.5 JCR Q4 In Chinese with English Abstract
    Abstract

    This study investigated soybean yield estimation by coupling the CASA (Carnegie–Ames–Stanford Approach) carbon assimilation model with the WOFOST (World Food Studies) crop growth model. The coupled framework leverages CASA's ability to estimate net primary productivity from remote sensing data and WOFOST's mechanistic representation of crop phenology and biomass allocation to produce field-scale yield estimates. The method was evaluated in major soybean-growing regions, demonstrating improved estimation accuracy over individual models alone and providing a practical approach for regional agricultural monitoring.

    Zeng, Y.; Hao, D.; Park, T.; Zhu, P.; Huete, A.; Myneni, R.; Knyazikhin, Y.; Qi, J.; Nemani, R. R.; Li, F.; Huang, J.; Gao, Y.; Li, B.; Ji, F.; Köhler, P.; Frankenberg, C.; Berry, J. A.; & Chen, M.

    Structural complexity biases vegetation greenness measures
    Nature Ecology & Evolution 2023 Co-Author IF 19.1 JCR Q1
    [Media 1] [NASA] [Medium] [Phys.org] [Free version]
    Abstract

    Satellite-based vegetation greenness indices are widely used as proxies for canopy photosynthetic activity and leaf biochemistry, yet their accuracy is sensitive to canopy structural properties. This study demonstrated that structural complexity in vegetation—particularly shadow casting by canopy elements—creates systematic biases in greenness measures retrieved from multi-spectral satellites. Using radiative transfer modeling and observational data spanning diverse biomes, the authors showed that shadow fraction governs the magnitude and direction of these biases, causing greenness indices to diverge from true leaf biochemistry signals in structurally complex canopies. The findings have broad implications for interpreting satellite-derived vegetation monitoring products and correcting for structural confounders in global ecological analyses.

    Hotspot effect improves the ability of satellites to track terrestrial photosynthesis
    Remote Sensing of Environment 2025, 317, 114492 Co-Author IF 13.5 JCR Q1
    Abstract

    This study investigated how the hotspot effect—the backscatter enhancement of reflectance observed when the viewing angle aligns with the solar illumination direction—improves satellite-based tracking of terrestrial photosynthesis. By leveraging multi-angle remote sensing observations and accounting for bidirectional reflectance distribution function (BRDF) effects at the hotspot geometry, the authors demonstrated enhanced sensitivity to canopy structural and biochemical properties that govern photosynthetic capacity. The approach produced improved estimates of gross primary production across diverse vegetation biomes, highlighting the value of hotspot-geometry observations for future satellite mission design and vegetation monitoring algorithms.

    Li, F.; Zhu, Q.; Yuan, K.; Ji, F.; Paul, A.; Lee, P.; Radeloff, V. C.; Chen, M.

    Projecting Large Fires in the Western US With an Interpretable and Accurate Hybrid Machine Learning Method
    Earth's Future 2024 Co-Author IF 7.4 JCR Q1
    Abstract

    A hybrid machine learning model was developed to project large wildfires in the western United States, achieving an F1-score of 0.846 ± 0.012—surpassing process-driven fire danger indices and four commonly used ML models by up to 40% and 9%, respectively. Using explainable AI techniques, the study identified substantial structural differences across ML fire models, even among those with similar predictive accuracy. Strong compound effects from multiple climate variables related to evaporative demand, energy release component, temperature, and wind speed were revealed as key drivers of large fire dynamics. The findings underscore the importance of assessing structural integrity of models in addition to their accuracy, and highlight the need to address compound climate extremes when projecting wildfire risk in the western US.

    Aboveground biomass estimation of late-stage maize based on the WOFOST model and UAV observations
    Journal of Remote Sensing 2020, 24(11): 1403–1418 Co-Author IF 4.0 JCR Q2 In Chinese with English Abstract
    Abstract

    This study estimated aboveground biomass (AGB) of late-stage maize by integrating UAV (unmanned aerial vehicle) remote sensing observations with the WOFOST crop growth model. High-resolution UAV imagery acquired during the grain-filling stage was used to derive canopy structural metrics that constrained WOFOST model state variables through data assimilation. The combined approach captured spatial variability in biomass accumulation at sub-field scales, outperforming both UAV-based empirical regression and standalone WOFOST simulations. The results demonstrate the value of UAV observations for improving crop model estimates during critical late-season growth phases when yield potential is largely determined.

    Crop Classification for UAV Visible Imagery Using Deep Semantic Segmentation Methods
    Geocarto International 2022, 1–23 Co-Author IF 3.8 JCR Q2
    Abstract

    Research on hyper-spectral remote sensing image classification by applying stacked de-noising auto-encoders neural network
    Multimedia Tools and Applications 2021(5) Co-Author IF 3.6 JCR Q2
    Abstract

    This study applied stacked de-noising auto-encoders (SDAE), an unsupervised deep learning architecture, to hyperspectral remote sensing image classification. The SDAE framework learns robust spectral feature representations by reconstructing corrupted input spectra, effectively capturing latent structure in high-dimensional hyperspectral data while reducing sensitivity to noise. The learned features were subsequently used to train a supervised classifier, achieving improved classification accuracy compared to traditional dimensionality reduction approaches such as PCA and standard shallow neural networks. The method demonstrated strong performance on benchmark hyperspectral datasets and offers a practical framework for land cover classification with limited labeled training samples.

    Mao, H.; Meng, J.; Ji, F.; Zhang, Q.; Fang, H.

    Comparison of Machine Learning Regression Algorithms for Cotton Leaf Area Index Retrieval Using Sentinel-2 Spectral Bands
    Applied Sciences 2019, 9, 1459 Co-Author IF 2.7 JCR Q2
    Abstract

    This study benchmarked five machine learning regression algorithms—Gradient Boosted Regression Trees (GBRT), Support Vector Regression (SVR), Artificial Neural Networks (ANN), Gaussian Process Regression (GPR), and Random Forest (RF)—for retrieving cotton leaf area index (LAI) using Sentinel-2 multispectral bands. GBRT achieved the highest overall accuracy (average R² = 0.854), while SVR demonstrated superior computational efficiency. GBRT also showed the most robust performance across varying training sample sizes, maintaining strong accuracy metrics (R² = 0.884) at reduced sample sizes. ANN performed best when fewer spectral bands were available, while SVR, GPR, and RF provided more stable performance across different band configurations. All five models demonstrated significant potential for practical cotton LAI retrieval in precision crop management applications.

    Global Forest Edge and its Dynamics in the 21st Century
    Under Review — Nature Communications Co-Author 2025
    Abstract

    Manuscript under review. This study quantifies global forest edge extent and its temporal dynamics throughout the 21st century using satellite-derived land cover products. Forest edges—the transition zones between forested and non-forested areas—have distinct microclimatic, ecological, and biodiversity properties compared to forest interiors. By tracking how edge extent has changed in response to deforestation, fragmentation, and reforestation globally, this work provides new insights into the conservation implications of forest structural change at planetary scales.
